## 2. BIOSYNTHETIC PATHWAY MAP

### The Universal Terpenoid Precursor System

```
CELLULAR BUILDING BLOCKS (Acetyl-CoA, Glycolysis Products)
          ↓
    ┌─────┴─────┐
    ↓           ↓
  MVA Pathway   MEP/DOXP Pathway
  (cytosolic)   (plastidial)
    ↓           ↓
    └─────┬─────┘
          ↓
    IPP/DMAPP (C5 isoprene units)
          ↓
    ┌─────┴──────────┬───────────┐
    ↓                ↓           ↓
   GPP (C10)       FPP (C15)    Shikimic Acid
    ↓                ↓           ↓
  MONOTERPENES    SESQUITERPENES  PHENYLPROPANOIDS
```

### Monoterpene Pathway (MEP → GPP → Products)

**GPP Derivatives — All Monoterpenes (C₁₀H₁₆ ± O):**

| Terpene | Enzyme | Subclass | Pathway Feature |
|---------|--------|----------|-----------------|
| Myrcene | Myrcene synthase | Acyclic | Direct deprotonation (no cyclization) |
| d-Limonene | Limonene synthase | Monocyclic | 6-membered ring cyclization |
| α-Pinene | Pinene synthase | Bicyclic | 6-ring + 4-ring (strained cyclobutane) |
| Terpinolene | Terpinolene synthase | Monocyclic | 6-membered cyclization; unstable (isomerizes) |
| β-Ocimene | Ocimene synthase | Acyclic | Triene structure (3 conjugated C=C) |
| Linalool | Linalool synthase | Acyclic | Alcohol-containing precursor; olfactory active |
| 1,8-Cineole | Cineole synthase | Bicyclic-oxide | α-Terpineol precursor; ether bridge formation |
| Menthol | 8-step pathway from limonene | Monocyclic | Via (-)-limonene → 8 reductase steps |
| Citronellol | Geraniol reductase | Acyclic | Geraniol parent → saturation of C2=C3 bond |

---

### Sesquiterpene Pathway (MVA → FPP → Products)

**FPP Derivatives — All Sesquiterpenes (C₁₅H₂₄ ± O):**

| Terpene | Enzyme | Cyclization | Product Type |
|---------|--------|-------------|--------------|
| β-Caryophyllene | CsTPS9FN (Cannabis) | Bicyclization | Strained 4-ring + 9-ring bicyclic |
| α-Humulene | Same synthase as BCP | Monocyclization | 11-membered ring (ring-opened isomer of BCP) |
| α-Bisabolol | Bisabolol synthase (BBS) | Monocyclization | Oxygenated alcohol product |
| β-Farnesene | Farnesene synthase | None (acyclic) | Simplest sesquiterpene: direct deprotonation only |

---

### Phenylpropanoid Pathway (Shikimic Acid Origin)

**Non-Terpenoid (Despite Presence in Essential Oils):**

```
Shikimic Acid → Chorismate → Phenylalanine
                                  ↓
                            Cinnamic Acid
                                  ↓
                            Coumaric Acid
                                  ↓
                        p-Coumaryl Alcohol
                                  ↓
                        Coniferyl Alcohol
                                  ↓
                    Eugenol Synthase + O-Methyltransferase
                                  ↓
                              EUGENOL
```

**Key Point:** Eugenol is biochemically distinct from all C₁₀/C₁₅ terpenes; different biosynthetic origin explains unique pharmacology.

## 4. EVIDENCE TIER RANKING

### Strength of Human Clinical Evidence by Terpene

#### **TIER 1: Strong Human RCTs (Randomized, Controlled, Double-Blind)**

1. **d-Limonene**
   - **Study:** Spindle et al. 2024 (Johns Hopkins)
   - **Evidence:** Double-blind, placebo-controlled, 20 healthy adults (n=12 extended), vaporized dosing
   - **Finding:** Dose-dependent anxiety reduction (15 mg THC + 5 mg limonene reduced anxiety vs. THC alone)
   - **Mechanism:** Selective anxiolytic without compromising THC therapeutic effects
   - **Patent:** PCT/US2022/014296 filed

2. **1,8-Cineole**
   - **Study:** Moss & Oliver 2012
   - **Evidence:** 20 healthy volunteers, rosemary oil aroma during cognitive testing
   - **Finding:** Plasma 1,8-cineole concentration directly correlates with objective cognitive performance (serial subtraction, visual processing)
   - **Mechanism:** Proven BBB penetration; serum kinetics documented

#### **TIER 2: Some Human Data (Open-Label, Small n, or Limited Controls)**

1. **Myrcene**
   - **Evidence Base:** Traditional use (lemongrass, verbena) + animal pharmacology support anxiolytic claims
   - **Limitation:** No double-blind human RCT; only animal data on barbiturate potentiation (Vale et al. 2002)

2. **Linalool**
   - **Evidence Base:** Lavender clinical literature (anxiety, sleep) + mechanistic animal studies (Harada 2018)
   - **Clinical Studies:** Multiple lavender RCTs (Cerebrum, phytotherapy journals) but not isolated linalool dosing
   - **Limitation:** Confounding with other lavender compounds (β-myrcene, limonene, caryophyllene in oil)

3. **Menthol**
   - **Evidence Base:** Extensive clinical literature for topical analgesia, IBS symptom relief
   - **Studies:** Peppermint oil RCTs (IBS, tension headache) but isolated menthol dosing limited
   - **Limitation:** Most human data on peppermint essential oil (multicomponent), not menthol alone

4. **α-Pinene**
   - **Evidence Base:** Forest bathing (shinrin-yoku) RCTs showing immune enhancement (NK cells sustained 30 days)
   - **Limitation:** Whole forest VOC mixture, not isolated pinene; attribution inferred from phytochemistry

#### **TIER 3: Animal Models Only (In Vivo, Rodent/Mammalian)**

1. **β-Caryophyllene**
   - **Studies:** ~50+ peer-reviewed animal pharmacology papers
   - **Models:** CB2 agonism confirmed in mice/rats; pain, inflammation, anxiety models
   - **Limitation:** No human RCT for pain or anxiety endpoints yet published

2. **Linalool** (supplementary)
   - **Animal Studies:** Elevated plus maze (EPM), light-dark box, forced swim test (FST) in mice
   - **Finding:** Anxiolytic and sedative-like effects; GABA-A dependency proven via flumazenil antagonism

3. **Terpinolene**
   - **Animal Studies:** Sedation (Ito & Ito 2013), antioxidant, anti-proliferative assays
   - **Limitation:** Fewer than 60 total publications; mixed evidence on sedation vs. energizing in humans

4. **α-Bisabolol**
   - **Animal Studies:** Pain, inflammation, wound healing models
   - **Limitation:** <30 peer-reviewed pharmacology papers; primarily in vitro enzyme assays

5. **β-Farnesene**
   - **Animal Studies:** Mast cell stabilization (allergic models); aphid ecology
   - **Limitation:** No mammalian pain/anxiety models; ecological/agricultural focus

6. **α-Humulene**
   - **Studies:** Anti-inflammatory (NF-κB), appetite suppression models
   - **Limitation:** Primarily in vitro assays; limited in vivo mammalian data

#### **TIER 4: In Vitro / Cell Culture / Limited Preclinical Data**

1. **Myrcene** (additional)
   - **In Vitro:** TRPV1 agonism (calcium imaging); opioid receptor binding

2. **d-Limonene** (additional)
   - **In Vitro:** COX-2 inhibition (IC₅₀ not specified for limonene alone); Ras prenylation inhibition

3. **β-Ocimene**
   - **Data:** Anti-inflammatory (NF-κB), antifungal (zone of inhibition)
   - **Limitation:** Mainly antibacterial screening; no mammalian pharmacology

4. **Eugenol**
   - **In Vitro:** TRP channel agonism (patch-clamp electrophysiology); Na+ channel blockade; COX-2/5-LOX inhibition
   - **Limitation:** Concentration-response curves in isolated cells; human topical dosing limited evidence

5. **Citronellol**
   - **Data:** Olfactory receptor binding (hOR1A2, hOR11D1)
   - **Limitation:** <20 publications; primarily fragrance chemistry; no pharmacology studies

#### **TIER 5: Theoretical / Computational Only**

1. **β-Farnesene** (theoretical mechanistic support)
   - Data primarily computational docking or ecological semiochemical function

#### **The Rotundone Connection: KEY BRIDGE**

**Rotundone (C₁₅H₂₄O — sesquiterpene ketone):** Structurally related to β-caryophyllene; directly responsible for "pepper" descriptor in Shiraz wines. Recent wine chemistry research shows rotundone as the molecular basis for sensory peppery notes perceived identically in both wine and cannabis. This creates a **direct sensory bridge**: wine tasting protocols for "peppery" notes can be applied to cannabis chemotype selection.

## 7. PRE-LOADING PROTOCOL RANKING

### Terpenes Ranked by Pre-Loading Suitability

#### **TIER A: BEST CANDIDATES (Strongest Mechanistic + Clinical Support)**

1. **d-Limonene** ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
   - **Strength:** Only human RCT evidence (Spindle 2024); proven anxiolytic selectivity
   - **Mechanism:** A2A agonism (adenosine receptors in striatum/limbic); GABA + dopamine upregulation
   - **Optimal Botanical Vehicle:** Pure lemon oil, Sauvignon Blanc aromatics, citrus peel oil
   - **Timing:** 15-30 min pre-consumption; rapid BBB penetration kinetics
   - **Dosing:** 1-5 mg limonene (equivalent to high-limonene strain concentration)
   - **Safety:** Extensive human safety data; GRAS status (Generally Recognized As Safe)
   - **Caveat:** Requires active olfactory route (nasal inhalation) for A2A priming

2. **β-Caryophyllene** ⭐⭐⭐⭐
   - **Strength:** CB2 selectivity (Ki=155 nM); extensive animal inflammation data
   - **Mechanism:** CB2-mediated NF-κB suppression (independent of THC); complementary to cannabinoids
   - **Optimal Botanical Vehicle:** Black pepper essential oil, clove oil (mixed caryophyllene + eugenol)
   - **Timing:** 15-30 min; rapid sesquiterpene absorption into lipophilic tissues
   - **Dosing:** 1-3 mg (equivalent to 1-3% of strain volatile profile)
   - **Safety:** Excellent; food-grade concentrations
   - **Advantage:** Works synergistically with THC/CBD without interfering; unique receptor target

3. **Myrcene** ⭐⭐⭐⭐
   - **Strength:** Proven GABA-A potentiation (barbiturate sleeptime extended 2.6×); animal anxiolytic models
   - **Mechanism:** Allosteric GABA-A enhancement; μ/δ opioid engagement peripherally
   - **Optimal Botanical Vehicle:** Lemongrass oil, verbena, Hallertau hops oil
   - **Timing:** 20-30 min (slower sesquiterpene absorption than monoterpenes)
   - **Dosing:** 2-5 mg (high myrcene strains contain 0.5-1.5%)
   - **Safety:** Food additive history; no toxicity documented
   - **Caveat:** Limited human data; relying on animal model extrapolation

4. **Linalool** ⭐⭐⭐⭐
   - **Strength:** Olfactory GABA-A activation proven (Harada 2018); lavender clinical tradition
   - **Mechanism:** Olfactory epithelium → piriform cortex → amygdala GABA enhancement
   - **Optimal Botanical Vehicle:** Lavender essential oil (R-linalool enantiomer preferred), lavender hydrosol
   - **Timing:** 10-20 min (rapid olfactory activation; shorter than systemic absorption)
   - **Dosing:** Aroma alone at detection threshold sufficient (~1-2 mg equivalent)
   - **Safety:** Extremely safe; culinary herb
   - **Critical Note:** Enantiomer matters — R-linalool (lavender) 9× more potent olfactorily than S-linalool

#### **TIER B: GOOD CANDIDATES (Plausible Mechanisms, Some Clinical Support)**

5. **1,8-Cineole** ⭐⭐⭐
   - **Strength:** Human cognitive enhancement correlate (Moss & Oliver); BBB penetration proven
   - **Mechanism:** Acetylcholinesterase inhibition (cognitive); respiratory mucosa effects
   - **Optimal Botanical Vehicle:** Rosemary ct. cineole (35-55% cineole), eucalyptus oil
   - **Timing:** 15-25 min (lipophilic; reliable BBB crossing)
   - **Dosing:** 2-5 mg (rosemary oil contains 10-20% by weight)
   - **Caveat:** Human cognitive benefit; anxiety/pain modulation less clear
   - **Strength:** Potential memory enhancement during cannabis use (unusual benefit)

6. **Menthol** ⭐⭐⭐
   - **Strength:** Extensive clinical data for pain/IBS (peppermint oil RCTs); TRPM8 selectivity
   - **Mechanism:** Cold receptor agonism → analgesic desensitization; TRPV1 cross-inhibition
   - **Optimal Botanical Vehicle:** Peppermint oil (30-50% menthol), Mentha arvensis oil (70-90%)
   - **Timing:** 20-30 min (high boiling point 212°C requires sustained inhalation)
   - **Dosing:** 2-4 mg (peppermint leaf infusion or oil vapor)
   - **Caveat:** Solid at room temperature (36-42°C melting point); requires warming for volatilization
   - **Strength:** Unique cooling + analgesic dual action

7. **α-Pinene** ⭐⭐⭐
   - **Strength:** AChE inhibition (memory enhancement); forest bathing NK cell immune data
   - **Mechanism:** Acetylcholinesterase inhibition (potentially counters THC memory impairment); BDNF pathway
   - **Optimal Botanical Vehicle:** Rosemary oil (15-25%), pine essential oil, conifer needle oil
   - **Timing:** 20-30 min (slower volatilization; monoterpene but bicyclic rigidity)
   - **Dosing:** 1-3 mg (forest air contains 1-5 ppb; typical cannabis strain 0.5-2%)
   - **Caveat:** Indirect evidence from forest ecology; not isolated compound studies
   - **Strength:** Neuroprotection angle (unique among terpenes)

#### **TIER C: SPECULATIVE (Limited Data, Theoretical Mechanisms)**

8. **Terpinolene** ⭐⭐
   - **Evidence:** Animal sedation (contradicts "sativa" reputation); antioxidant in vitro
   - **Mechanism:** GABA-A enhancement (paradoxical with aroma profile); oxidative stress reduction
   - **Botanical Vehicle:** Tea tree oil (1-5%), nutmeg oil
   - **Caveat:** THE "Terpinolene Paradox" — energizing aroma but sedative pharmacology unresolved
   - **Recommendation:** Experimental; requires human validation

9. **α-Humulene** ⭐⭐
   - **Evidence:** NF-κB suppression (in vitro); CB1 engagement at high doses
   - **Mechanism:** Anti-inflammatory complementary to BCP
   - **Botanical Vehicle:** Hops oil (10-30% humulene), black pepper
   - **Limitation:** Always co-occurs with BCP in nature; hard to isolate mechanistically
   - **Recommendation:** Use as "humulene-enriched" pre-load with BCP vehicles

10. **α-Bisabolol** ⭐⭐
    - **Evidence:** German chamomile tradition; GABA-A modulation suggested but limited
    - **Mechanism:** Anti-inflammatory; possible GABA-A allosteric site
    - **Botanical Vehicle:** German chamomile tea/oil (25-50% bisabolol)
    - **Limitation:** High boiling point (313°C) = poor volatility; inhalation delivery challenging
    - **Recommendation:** Oral pre-load (tea) or sustained diffusion (diffuser 30 min before)

#### **TIER D: CHALLENGING (Delivery Issues, Limited Evidence, Speculative)**

11. **β-Ocimene** ⭐
    - **Evidence:** Anti-inflammatory in vitro; antifungal screening
    - **Limitation:** Extreme volatility (oxidizes rapidly); unstable formulation
    - **Mechanism:** NF-κB modulation (theoretical)
    - **Botanical Vehicle:** Fresh plant material only (oil degrades); minimal commercial prep
    - **Not Recommended:** Stability/delivery constraints exceed benefit potential

12. **Eugenol** ⭐
    - **Evidence:** TRP channel agonism (TRPV1/TRPA1); local anesthetic potential
    - **Mechanism:** Numbing effect could reduce inhalation irritation; polymodal nociception priming
    - **Botanical Vehicle:** Clove bud oil (72-90% eugenol); extremely pungent
    - **Limitation:** High boiling point (254°C); irritating at high concentration; not anxiolytic
    - **Not Recommended:** Irritant potential outweighs benefits for most users

13. **β-Farnesene** ⭐
    - **Evidence:** Mast cell interactions (allergy); green apple allergen
    - **Mechanism:** Anti-allergic potential (theoretical); antioxidant
    - **Limitation:** Minimal human data; trace in most botanical sources
    - **Not Recommended:** Insufficient evidence; unstable (oxidizes)

14. **Citronellol** ⭐
    - **Evidence:** Olfactory receptor binding (hOR1A2); rose oil tradition
    - **Mechanism:** Pleasant aroma; antioxidant support (theoretical)
    - **Limitation:** <20 papers; no pharmacology; low volatility
    - **Not Recommended:** Insufficient evidence for pharmacological pre-loading

## 8. MOLECULAR COUSIN NETWORK

### Key Structural Relationships & Metabolic Pathways

#### **ISOMERIC RELATIONSHIPS**

```
CONSTITUTIONAL ISOMERS (Same formula, different structure):
├─ β-Caryophyllene ↔ α-Humulene (C₁₅H₂₄)
│  └─ BCP: Bicyclic (4-ring + 9-ring); CB2 selective
│  └─ Humulene: Monocyclic (11-ring); CB1 weak agonist
│  └─ Co-occur from same synthase (CsTPS9FN); variable ratio
│
├─ Myrcene ↔ β-Ocimene (C₁₀H₁₆)
│  └─ Myrcene: Acyclic conjugated diene (C1=C2-C3=C4)
│  └─ Ocimene: Acyclic conjugated triene (C1=C2-C3=C4-C5=C6)
│  └─ Ocimene is "one C=C richer" than myrcene
│
├─ Menthol ↔ Neomenthol ↔ Isomenthol (C₁₀H₂₀O, stereoisomers)
│  └─ Identical molecular formula; 8 stereoisomeric variants
│  └─ (-)-Menthol (1R,2S,5R) is natural, active form
│  └─ Produced from same (-)-limonene pathway via 8-step synthesis
│
├─ Geraniol ↔ Nerol (C₁₀H₁₈O, geometric isomers)
│  └─ Both acyclic monoterpenols; differ only in C2=C3 double bond geometry
│  └─ Geraniol: (E) configuration at C2=C3 (trans)
│  └─ Nerol: (Z) configuration at C2=C3 (cis)
│  └─ Linalool is an allylic isomer (OH position at C3 instead of C1)
```

#### **REDUCTION PATHWAY: Terpenol Hierarchy**

```
GERANIOL (C₁₀H₁₈O, unsaturated C2=C3)
    ↓ [Geraniol reductase; Old Yellow Enzyme]
    ↓ [Reduction of C2=C3 double bond]
CITRONELLOL (C₁₀H₂₀O, saturated C2-C3)
    ↓ [Further reduction/isomerization in specific tissues]
    ↓ [Plant/enzyme-specific modifications]
ROSE ALCOHOLS & PHYTOL ANALOGS

CASCADE DETAIL:
• Geraniol → Citronellol: Direct saturation (simplest reduction)
• Citronellol → Menthol: Requires cyclization + further reduction (8-step pathway via limonene parent)
```

#### **OXIDATION CASCADE: Functional Group Elaboration**

```
ALDEHYDES (C=O at terminal position):
  Geranial (geraniol oxidized)
  Citronellal (citronellol oxidized)

    ↓ [Aldehyde reductase]
    ↓

PRIMARY ALCOHOLS (CH₂OH):
  Geraniol, Citronellol (most common in essential oils)

    ↓ [Secondary oxidation routes]
    ↓

KETONES / SECONDARY ALCOHOLS (C=O in ring):
  Menthone → (-)-Menthol [via reductase, Step 7 in menthol synthesis]
  Pulegone (toxic intermediate in menthol pathway)
```

#### **THE LIMONENE METABOLIC HUB: Divergent Pathways from Single Parent**

```
GPP (Geranyl Pyrophosphate)
    ↓ [Limonene synthase]
    ↓
(-)-LIMONENE (C₁₀H₁₆, monocyclic; parental structure)
    │
    ├─→ PATH 1: Oxidation [Limonene-3-hydroxylase]
    │           ↓
    │       (-)-trans-Isopiperitenol
    │           ↓ [8 enzymatic steps]
    │           ↓
    │       (-)-MENTHOL ← "LIMONENE-DERIVED"
    │
    ├─→ PATH 2: Further oxygenation [P450 oxidases]
    │           ↓
    │       Menthol isomers
    │       Menthofuran (competing pathway)
    │
    └─→ PATH 3: Direct α-terpineol formation
                ↓
            [Terpene synthase rearrangement]
                ↓
            1,8-CINEOLE formation
```

**Key Insight:** Menthol and 1,8-cineole are both "downstream oxidation products" of the same (-)-limonene parent. This explains structural similarity despite different pharmacology. Industrial pinene conversion to limonene is also feasible via isomerization, suggesting potential cross-reactivity in botanical preparations.

#### **SESQUITERPENE PRECURSOR DIVERGENCE: FPP → Three Major Sesquiterpenes**

```
FPP (Farnesyl Diphosphate) ← MVA pathway converges here
    │
    ├─→ [No cyclization; direct deprotonation]
    │   ↓
    │   β-FARNESENE (acyclic sesquiterpene)
    │   • Simplest sesquiterpene synthase reaction
    │   • No ring closure required
    │
    ├─→ [1,11-Cyclization via Humulene synthase]
    │   ↓
    │   E,E-HUMULYL CATION
    │   ↓ [Deprotonation, no rearrangement]
    │   ↓
    │   α-HUMULENE (monocyclic, 11-membered ring)
    │
    ├─→ [1,11-Cyclization → Post-rearrangement carbocation shift]
    │   ↓
    │   E,E-HUMULYL CATION
    │   ↓ [Complex rearrangement; bicyclization]
    │   ↓
    │   β-CARYOPHYLLENE (bicyclic; 4-ring + 9-ring)
    │   • Strained cyclobutane ring
    │   • Most thermodynamically stable sesquiterpene product
    │
    └─→ [Ester reduction + cyclization pathway]
        ↓
        α-BISABOLOL (monocyclic sesquiterpene alcohol)
        • Contains hydroxyl group
        • Biosynthetic branch via bisabolol synthase
```

**Key Mechanism:** The **same enzyme (CsTPS9FN in cannabis)** catalyzes both humulene AND β-caryophyllene formation. The ratio depends on:
- Substrate availability (FPP concentration)
- Competing substrate-directed cyclization mechanisms
- Post-cyclization rearrangement efficiency
- Kinetic vs. thermodynamic product selectivity

**Implication:** BCP and humulene pre-loading are mechanistically linked; isolating humulene effects is challenging because both are naturally co-produced.

#### **THE WINE BRIDGE: Rotundone as BCP Isomer**

```
β-CARYOPHYLLENE (C₁₅H₂₄, sesquiterpene hydrocarbon)
    │
    ├─→ [Enzymatic oxidation to ketone; may occur in wine aging]
    │   ↓
    │   ROTUNDONE (C₁₅H₂₄O, sesquiterpene ketone)
    │   • 13-membered ring configuration
    │   • Responsible for "pepper" descriptor in Shiraz wines
    │   • Structural isomer / oxidized analog of BCP
    │   • Same sensory modality: PEPPER

SENSORY BRIDGE:
"Peppery" descriptor in wine (rotundone/sesquiterpene ketone)
                    ↓ [Structural homology]
"Peppery" descriptor in cannabis (β-caryophyllene)
                    ↓
Wine tasting notes inform cannabis chemotype selection
```

## 9. KEY CITATIONS COMPENDIUM

### The 30 Most Important Papers Across All Terpenes

#### **LANDMARK HUMAN CLINICAL STUDIES**

1. **Spindle TR, et al. (2024)** "Acute effects of d-limonene on cannabis-induced anxiety: a randomized, placebo-controlled trial."
   - *Neuropsychopharmacology* 49:1099–1106 [IF: 4.5]
   - **DOI:** 10.1038/s41386-024-01879-0
   - **PubMed:** 38498958
   - **Significance:** ONLY human RCT showing terpene-cannabinoid interaction at anxiolytic level
   - **Key Finding:** 15 mg THC + 5 mg limonene significantly reduced anxiety vs. THC alone; limonene alone had no effect

2. **Moss M, Oliver L (2012)** "Plasma 1,8-cineole correlates with cognitive performance following exposure to rosemary essential oil aroma."
   - *Therapeutic Advances in Psychopharmacology* 2(3):103–113
   - **PMC:** 3736918
   - **Significance:** Proven BBB penetration of terpene; direct correlation between plasma levels and objective cognitive performance
   - **Key Finding:** 1,8-cineole > 75 ng/mL plasma level → significant improvement in serial subtraction and visual processing speed

3. **Vale TG, et al. (2002)** "Central depressant effects of citral, myrcene and limonene, constituents of essential oils."
   - *Phytotherapy Research* 16(4):367–372
   - **DOI:** 10.1002/ptr.1090
   - **Significance:** Landmark animal pharmacology; barbiturate potentiation data (myrcene 200 mg/kg = 2.6× sleep time increase)
   - **Key Finding:** Myrcene, limonene, citral all enhance GABA-A signaling; dose-dependent CNS depression

#### **CANNABINOID-TERPENE INTERACTION STUDIES**

4. **Gertsch J, et al. (2008)** "β-Caryophyllene is a dietary cannabinoid."
   - *Proceedings of the National Academy of Sciences* 105(26):9099–9104
   - **DOI:** 10.1073/pnas.0803601105
   - **Significance:** Landmark CB2 selectivity paper; established BCP as selective CB2 agonist (Ki = 155 nM)
   - **Key Finding:** BCP is first documented dietary compound directly activating CB2 without CB1 cross-reactivity

5. **Komiya M, et al. (2006)** "Lemon oil volatiles have anxiety- and sedative-like effects on mice."
   - *Phytomedicine* 13(8):540–546
   - **DOI:** 10.1016/j.phymed.2006.01.013
   - **Significance:** Demonstrates linalool/limonene anxiolytic effects through GABA and serotonergic mechanisms in vivo

6. **Harada H, et al. (2018)** "Linalool odor-induced anxiolytic effects in mice."

## CONCLUSION

This master reference synthesizes the complete current knowledge base across 14 major cannabis terpenes. The evidence hierarchy clearly demonstrates:

1. **d-Limonene** represents the gold standard for pre-loading with human RCT support
2. **β-Caryophyllene** offers unique CB2 selectivity complementary to cannabinoids
3. **Myrcene** and **Linalool** provide GABA-A modulatory support with animal model backing
4. **Emerging candidates** (α-pinene, 1,8-cineole, menthol) show promise but require human validation

The cross-botanical matrix enables practical implementation: lemon oil for d-limonene anxiety reduction, black pepper for β-caryophyllene pre-loading, lavender for olfactory GABA-A priming, and rosemary for cognitive enhancement via 1,8-cineole and α-pinene.

**Next Steps for Research:**
- Human RCTs for myrcene, linalool, α-pinene anxiolytic/cognitive endpoints
- Terpene-cannabinoid pharmacokinetic interaction studies
- Enantiomeric specificity validation (R vs. S-linalool; (+) vs. (−)-menthol)
- Pre-loading protocol optimization (timing, dosing, botanical vehicle selectivity)
- Solful strain × terpene mapping (see companion document for 24-strain analysis)

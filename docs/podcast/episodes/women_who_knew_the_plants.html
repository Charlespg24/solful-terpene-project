The Women Who Knew the Plants

How the world's first pharmacologists were erased — and how the molecules are proving them right

I

The Original Pharmacologists

Before universities, before guilds, before pharmaceutical patents — before anyone had the word for what they were doing — women were the pharmacologists. Every culture, every continent, every era. The pattern is global because the logic is simple: women attended birth, illness, and death. Birth, illness, and death are where the body is most pharmacologically demanding. The women closest to those moments accumulated the most precise knowledge of how plants interacted with the body.

The earliest named female physician in recorded history is Peseshet, inscribed at Giza around 2400 BCE as "Lady Overseer of the Female Physicians" — a title implying she supervised an entire corps of women practitioners in ancient Egypt's Fourth Dynasty. In Rome, midwifery was a stratified, respected profession with named practitioners like Aspasia, who developed techniques for turning breech fetuses. In 12th-century China, the physician Tan Yunxian compiled 31 case reports on menstrual disorders and habitual abortion — the only surviving physician's book by a woman in the Ming Dynasty. In her preface, she wrote: "I beg readers' indulgence and ask that they do not laugh at me."

The knowledge lived primarily in oral transmission — passed mother to daughter, healer to apprentice, in kitchens and gardens and birthing rooms. It did not live in texts because women did not have access to the institutions that produced texts. That asymmetry — men's knowledge written down, women's knowledge memorized — would prove catastrophic.

The phrase "old wives' tale" is a cultural category designed to delegitimize female empirical knowledge. But the molecules do not care about the gender of the person who first identified their effects.
The pharmacological record

Here is the pharmacological irony that runs through all of this: the women were right. A landmark 2001 study by Fabricant and Farnsworth examined 122 plant-derived drugs currently in global clinical use and found that 80% of them have an ethnomedical use identical or closely related to what they're prescribed for today. Eighty percent. Those are not coincidences. That is the accuracy rate of an empirical science conducted across millennia by people who had no receptors, no double-blind trials, and no institutional funding — just careful, multigenerational observation of what happened when you gave a plant to a body.

II

What the Plants Actually Did

Let's be precise about what knowledge these women held. Not superstition. Not folk belief. Pharmacology — conducted empirically, without the vocabulary, but with the results.

Lavender
Lavandula angustifolia

The folk remedy for sleep and anxiety. In 2018, Harada et al. confirmed its primary molecule, linalool, activates GABA-A receptors via the olfactory system — the same pathway as Valium, blocked by the same antidote. Human RCT In Germany today, standardized lavender oil (Silexan) is an approved pharmaceutical that matched lorazepam in head-to-head randomized trials.

Yarrow
Achillea millefolium

Named for the legend that Achilles used it on battlefield wounds. Modern pharmacology confirmed it: yarrow reduces bleeding time by 36% through sesquiterpene lactones (achillicin) and flavonoids that inhibit enzymes driving inflammation. Every medieval healer who packed a wound with yarrow was deploying a verified hemostatic agent. Animal + in vitro

Cannabis
Cannabis sativa / indica

Documented in women's medicine for over 5,000 years: labor pain, dysmenorrhea, postpartum recovery. Assyrian tablets cite it for colic and childbirth. The British Medical Journal declared in 1883 that "no medicine gives such good results" for menorrhagia. It remained in the U.S. Pharmacopeia until 1942 — prescribed by mainstream physicians for women's pain.

Sweet Wormwood
Artemisia annua

Used in Chinese folk medicine for fever for 2,000 years. In 1971, pharmacologist Tu Youyou read a 4th-century woman's medical text, recognized a cold-water extraction instruction, and isolated artemisinin — saving over 200 million lives from malaria. She received the 2015 Nobel Prize. The folk knowledge was right. It took 1,600 years for the institution to notice. Nobel Prize

The Woman Who Proved It All

Tu Youyou is known in China as the "Three-Without Scientist": no postgraduate degree, no overseas training, not a member of either national academy — having been rejected from the Chinese Academy of Sciences three times. She held no administrative rank. When she found the answer to malaria in a 4th-century text attributed to a woman physician's influence, she volunteered as the first human test subject for the extract she'd prepared. The Lasker Foundation called artemisinin "arguably the most important pharmaceutical intervention of the last half century." Her Nobel Prize is not just for a molecule. It is a vindication of an entire epistemological tradition.

III

The Machinery of Erasure

The suppression of women's botanical knowledge was not a single event. It was a cascading system built across centuries by interlocking institutions, each one reinforcing the others.

It began with universities. The founding of European medical schools — Bologna (1088), Paris (1274), Montpellier — transformed medicine from a distributed craft into a credentialed profession. These institutions grew from cathedral schools; students took holy orders; only men could be ordained. No explicit prohibition against women was always needed. The requirement itself served as an absolute barrier. Latin literacy — the language of learned medicine — became male by structural default.

The clearest window into how this worked is the trial of Jacoba Félicié, Paris, 1322. The University of Paris Medical Faculty prosecuted her for practicing medicine without a license. She presented eight witnesses who testified she had cured patients that licensed physicians had given up for dead. The court's verdict was astonishing in its naked logic:

Her plea that she cured many sick persons whom the aforesaid master could not cure ought not to stand and is frivolous, since it is certain that a man approved in the aforesaid art could cure better than any woman.
Paris Medical Faculty, 1322

She was fined sixty Parisian livres and threatened with excommunication. Not for incompetence. Not for harm. For healing too well while being the wrong gender.

Then came Hildegard von Bingen (1098–1179), the extraordinary counterexample. Her Physica documented 293 plants and over 2,000 remedies; her Causae et Curae addressed female anatomy, menstruation, and herbal emmenagogues with remarkable sympathy. She is celebrated. But understand why she survived: she was a Benedictine abbess, correspondent of popes and emperors, protected by the institutional walls of the cloister. Her knowledge had walls around it. The same knowledge in the hands of a village healer had no such protection — and that difference, over the following centuries, would become lethal.

IV

The Witch Trials & the Baptism Window

In 1486, Dominican friar Heinrich Kramer published the Malleus Maleficarum — "The Hammer of Witches." It went through 28 editions before 1600. Among its most explicit targets: midwives. "No one does more harm to the Catholic Faith than midwives," the text states flatly, devoting an entire chapter to "How Witch Midwives commit most Horrid Crimes."

The theological logic was specific. Catholic doctrine held that unbaptized babies who died were consigned to Limbo, unsaveable. Midwives controlled the critical window between birth and baptism. They were also the women most likely to provide herbal preparations for labor pain — which the Church's reading of Genesis 3:16 ("in pain thou shalt bring forth children") made theologically transgressive. Pain relief in childbirth was, within this framework, an interference with divine judgment.

What the Numbers Actually Show

Historian Brian Levack (University of Texas) estimated approximately 110,000 witch trials and 40,000–60,000 executions across 1450–1750. Approximately 75–80% of the accused were women. The worst persecutions occurred not in Spain or Italy — where centralized Inquisitions demanded evidence — but in the Holy Roman Empire's fragmented small territories, where local courts operated without appeal. The county of Bamberg alone executed approximately 1,000 people in five years. The Spanish Inquisition, by contrast, executed fewer than two dozen alleged witches in its entire history. Institutional standards mattered. Their absence was fatal.

And the botanical dimension was real. The "flying ointments" described in trial records — containing henbane, belladonna, mandrake, monkshood — were pharmacologically plausible. These plants contain tropane alkaloids (atropine, scopolamine) that are documented deliriants absorbed through the skin, producing vivid hallucinations including the sensation of flight. The Spanish royal physician Andrés de Laguna tested a preparation in 1545 and confirmed it produced deep trance and vivid dreamlike states. The women who used psychoactive plant preparations in folk ritual were not practicing magic. They were practicing ethnopharmacology. The institution that burned them did not make that distinction.

Careful historians have noted, rightly, that licensed midwives were generally protected by their community standing — it was the informal healer, the woman who provided reproductive services outside any sanctioned structure, who was most vulnerable. The horror of the witch trials was not a targeted campaign against pharmacologists. It was something broader and in some ways worse: a mass terror directed at women who held social power outside institutional channels, which botanical knowledge reliably conferred.

V

Cannabis: The Women's Medicine That Was Erased

Of all the plants in women's pharmacopoeia, the story of cannabis is the most precisely documented erasure — and the one with the most clearly identified perpetrator.

~3000 BCE — Assyria

Tablets mention cannabis over 30 times, "mainly against colic and labor pain." The Ebers Papyrus (1550 BCE) records cannabis-honey preparations for vaginal application.

1883 — British Medical Journal

"Indian hemp has such specific use in menorrhagia — there is no medicine which has given such good results." Cannabis was the third most common ingredient in compounded medicines, manufactured by Parke-Davis, Eli Lilly, and Squibb specifically for women's conditions.

1890 — The Lancet

Sir J. Russell Reynolds (Physician to the Royal Household) publishes: "When pure and administered carefully, [cannabis] is one of the most valuable medicines we possess" — recommending it for dysmenorrhea, migraine, and depression. Cannabis had been in the U.S. Pharmacopeia since 1850.

May 4, 1937 — Washington D.C.

Dr. William Woodward, AMA legislative counsel, testifies before Congress: "The American Medical Association knows of no evidence that marijuana is a dangerous drug." He warns that future investigation may show substantial medical uses. The committee attacks him for obstruction.

Days later — House Floor

Representative Fred Vinson states that the AMA gave the bill "full support and approval" — the exact opposite of what Woodward testified. The House debates for 90 seconds. The Marihuana Tax Act passes. Cannabis is gone from pharmacopoeias by 1942. A women's medicine used continuously for 5,000 years is criminalized in an afternoon.

The Method

Harry Anslinger, architect of prohibition, deliberately used the Mexican-associated term "marihuana" rather than "cannabis" to sever the public's recognition of the dangerous new drug from the familiar medicine. Renaming was erasure. Cannabis was the remedy in every grandmother's house. Marihuana was a foreign menace.

They renamed it so you wouldn't recognize what they were taking away.

VI

The Recovery: What Returning Looks Like

The suppression was not total. The knowledge survived the way it always survived: in women's networks, in practice, in the oral tradition that institutional erasure could wound but never fully destroy.

In 1978, Rosemary Gladstar founded the California School of Herbal Studies — now the oldest continuously operating herb school in the United States. The daughter of Armenian genocide survivors, she had learned botanical medicine from her grandmother. She would go on to found United Plant Savers, the New England Women's Herbal Conference, the International Herb Symposium, and co-found Traditional Medicinals. She is called the Godmother of American Herbalism. She frames every bit of it as reclamation: "These plants were women's medicine. They were stolen. We are taking them back."

Robin Wall Kimmerer — SUNY botanist, enrolled member of the Citizen Potawatomi Nation, 2022 MacArthur Fellow — published Braiding Sweetgrass in 2013, weaving Indigenous women's plant knowledge with Western botanical science. Named "Best Essay Collection of the Decade," it became one of the most borrowed titles in American public libraries, reaching millions of people who had never thought of plants as teachers.

And then there is the molecular validation that reframes everything. The GABA-A mechanism for lavender's anxiolytic effect was formally demonstrated in 2018. Yarrow's hemostatic compounds were quantified in peer-reviewed pharmacology journals in the 2020s. The 80% validation rate documented by Fabricant and Farnsworth is not the discovery of new knowledge. It is the belated institutional recognition of very old knowledge — knowledge accumulated through generations of women's empirical observation, dismissed as superstition for five centuries, and now proven right by the very science that replaced it.

The modern cannabis legalization movement — whatever its politics — created something unprecedented: a mandatory public analytical infrastructure for plant chemistry. Certificates of Analysis. Standardized terpene testing at accredited labs. QR codes on dispensary packaging showing exact molecular profiles. No herb in human history has ever been as analytically transparent as legal cannabis in a regulated state. Women are founding companies within that infrastructure at extraordinary rates, building businesses explicitly connected to the tradition of botanical healing that the 1937 Act tried to erase. The conversation, interrupted for 87 years, has resumed.

☽

The wise women were not practicing magic. They were practicing pharmacy. The institution that spent five hundred years dismissing them as superstitious has spent the last few decades proving them right — receptor by receptor, molecule by molecule, clinical trial by clinical trial.

Linalool modulates GABA-A whether a pharmacologist or a grandmother describes the observation. Artemisinin kills malaria whether the extraction instructions come from a 4th-century text or a 21st-century lab. What changes is who gets credit, and whose knowledge is allowed to survive.

The molecules were always telling the truth.

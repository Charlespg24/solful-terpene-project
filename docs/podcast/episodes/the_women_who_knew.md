### [COLD OPEN]

Paris, 1322. A woman named Jacqueline sits across from a panel of men she has never met. They are professors from the Faculty of Medicine at the University of Paris. She is not a student — women are not permitted to be students. She is not a colleague — women are not permitted to be colleagues. She is a healer. And today, she is on trial.

The charge: practicing medicine without a license. A license that, by design, requires university training. Training that, by design, excludes her. The loop is elegant. The outcome is predetermined.

Eight of her former patients have come to testify. A man named Odo, who she treated by sweating over coals and burning herbs, will tell the court she is wiser than any physician or surgeon in Paris. A woman named Bilbaut, who had been left for dead by licensed doctors, will say Jacqueline cured her completely. Seven others will say versions of the same thing: she healed us. The men you sent could not.

None of it will matter. The court will rule, and I'm quoting the verdict here, that "it is certain that a man approved in the aforesaid art could cure better than any woman." She will be excommunicated, fined sixty Parisian livres, and banned from ever touching a patient again.

This is not a story about one woman in medieval Paris. This is a story about what happened to an entire tradition of knowledge — who held it, who destroyed it, and why it matters that the molecules at the center of that tradition are the same ones we're rediscovering right now.

### [THE KNOWLEDGE]

Before there were pharmacies, before there were universities, before medicine was a profession with gates and gatekeepers — there were women with plants.

This isn't romanticizing. This is the historical record. For most of human civilization, healing was domestic labor, and domestic labor was women's work. The knowledge was oral. Mother to daughter. Grandmother to granddaughter. Neighbor to neighbor in a village where the nearest man who could read Latin was three days' ride away. Remedies were written in recipe books — collaborative, handwritten domestic manuals that combined cooking instructions with medical preparations — and they were passed down through generations, continually added to, never finished.

And the remedies worked. Not all of them, not perfectly, but enough of them that modern pharmacology keeps validating what these women somehow knew. Lavender for anxiety and wound care — we now understand that linalool modulates GABA-A receptors, the same mechanism as benzodiazepines. Rosemary for memory and mental clarity — alpha-pinene inhibits acetylcholinesterase, the enzyme that breaks down the memory neurotransmitter acetylcholine. Chamomile for fever and inflammation — bisabolol is a documented anti-inflammatory that shows up in peer-reviewed pharmacology journals. Peppermint for digestive distress — menthol activates TRPM8 cold receptors and relaxes smooth muscle in the gut.

These women didn't know the receptor names. They didn't need to. They had something better: centuries of accumulated observation, tested against the hardest dataset there is — real sick

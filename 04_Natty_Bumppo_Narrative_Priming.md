## Short Story: "The Frontiersman's Saturday"

Ellis hadn't owned an alarm clock in nineteen days.

He knew the number because he was still counting, the way you count the days of a vacation at the beginning, before the rhythm of not-working replaces the rhythm of working and the counting stops on its own. He was sixty-two and had taught seventh-grade history for thirty-four years at a middle school in Ukiah, and now he didn't, and the Saturday stretching out in front of him was identical to the Friday behind it and the Sunday ahead of it, and this fact — which should have terrified him, which his colleagues had warned him about, which every retirement book on the library shelf insisted was a crisis-in-waiting — felt instead like the first interesting thing that had happened to his body in years.

He was on the back porch of his place in Redwood Valley, a two-bedroom on three acres that he'd bought in 2004 when a history teacher could still afford land in Mendocino County. The porch faced east, toward the hills, and the September light was doing that thing it does in Northern California wine country — arriving at an angle that made everything look like it was posing for a photograph it didn't know was being taken. The oaks on the hillside were the dark green of late summer. The grass between them was gold. Somewhere a red-tailed hawk was doing slow circles above the drainage, hunting on autopilot.

The garden was done for the day. He'd spent the morning with the tomatoes — the last of the season, heavy on the vine, that particular shade of red that meant they'd be sauce by Wednesday. He'd weeded the squash. He'd stood in the rows and done nothing for a while, which was a new skill he was developing, standing in a garden without an agenda, and he was getting better at it.

Now: the porch. A wooden chair he'd bought at a yard sale in Hopland, the kind of Adirondack that forces you into a recline whether you intended one or not. His body had settled into it with the specific gravity of a man who'd done physical work that morning and had no physical work remaining. His boots were off. His feet were bare on the warm planks. His hands were dirty in the way that garden dirt is dirty — not grime but evidence.

The diffuser was running on the small table beside him — he'd set it up out here last week after realizing that the porch was where he spent most of his time now, and the extension cord reached. He'd mixed the blend that morning: black pepper, the backbone, fourteen drops that smelled like warmth had been given a postal code. Lemon, ten drops of a brightness so specific it was almost a sound — a clean, high note that cut through the dusty September air like a knife through soft fruit. Sage, six drops, and this was the one that got him, because the sage oil smelled exactly like the hillside behind his house, the one where the wild sage grew in thick grey-green bunches that his neighbor Sandra said she used to make smudge sticks. Lemongrass underneath, six drops of something green and soft that rounded the pepper's directness into something that could be inhaled without paying attention. And geranium — four drops, floral and faintly tart, like a garden that was also somehow a plum.

The black pepper arrived first, as it always did. It was the most forward of the oils, the one that didn't wait to be noticed. The warmth settled behind his sternum and moved outward like a small fire finding its radius. Then the lemon, clean and honest, and the sage, grounding everything to this specific hillside, this specific September, this specific man on this specific porch. The lemongrass softened the blend the way a creek softens a canyon — slowly, imperceptibly, by just being there. The geranium floated on top of it all, fruity and strange, a note that didn't explain itself and didn't need to.

From inside the house, the speaker was playing Bonobo — something with a slow, patient beat that sounded like the musical equivalent of watching clouds from a hammock. No lyrics. No hurry. Just texture moving through time.

The jar sat on the porch railing where he'd left it this morning. He'd gotten it from Josh, his neighbor a half-mile down the road. Josh Khan ran Moon Gazer Farms with his wife Sandra — a biodynamic operation, seed-sown, everything grown in the sun and under the moon. Ellis had watched them build the place over the past several years, watched them haul dead trees from the forest to make their own soil — hugelkulture, Josh called it, layering branches with native dirt and manure from their animals, mulching to hold the moisture. A closed-loop system. Nothing brought in from outside, nothing wasted. The chickens ate the snails and turned the shells into calcium in their droppings, which went back into the beds. Ellis admired it the way he admired anyone who'd figured out how to make a life that was also an ecosystem.

Josh had stopped by last week with a jar and a grin. "Natty Bumppo," he'd said, handing it over the fence.

"Like the Leatherstocking Tales?"

Josh had looked genuinely pleased. "You're the first person who's gotten that without me explaining."

"I taught American Lit for two years before I switched to history," Ellis said. "Cooper was terrible at dialogue but he understood something about being in the woods."

"That's exactly it," Josh had said. "That's this strain. It's like — you know how Natty Bumppo just moved through the forest without needing to own it? That's what this one does. It puts you in the landscape without making you do anything about it."

Now Ellis opened the jar and the smell met the September air — kerosene and musk and sour plum, three aromas that had no business working together and yet did, the way a good jazz trio works, each player listening to the others more than to themselves. The kerosene was sharp and almost mechanical, a smell that should have been industrial but somehow, in this context, was honest — like fuel before it's fuel, when it's still just the earth giving up something it's been holding for a million years. The musk was animal and warm. The sour plum was the surprise — tart and fruity and alive, cutting through the heavier notes the way his lemon oil cut through the pepper.

He packed the small clay pipe he'd had since the '90s — a one-hitter, nothing fancy, the glaze worn to a smooth matte by decades of thumbs. One draw. That was always enough when the porch was primed and the air was right and the garden was done.

The smoke tasted like the jar smelled — kerosene and plum and musk — and it met the diffuser's work in the warm September air, and the two blends recognized each other. The black pepper from the diffuser and the spicy warmth of the flower. The lemon oil and the tart plum. The sage from the bottle and the sage from the hillside and the sage from the flower itself, all three versions of the same conversation. The geranium's fruity float matched the sour plum's tartness, and suddenly the porch smelled like a place where every version of autumn was happening at once.

It came on easy. That was the thing about this one — it didn't arrive like a wave or a permission or a door opening. It arrived like a fact you'd forgotten and then remembered without urgency. Oh, right. I don't have to do anything. Not the anxious version of that sentence, not the existential-crisis version. The Saturday version. The version where there's tomato sauce to make on Wednesday and nothing between here and there but time, and time, it turns out, is not the enemy the school bell always made it seem.

His body did something it hadn't done in thirty-four years of standing in front of classrooms: it went genuinely, completely, purposelessly loose. Not tired. Not sedated. Loose. The difference was important. Tired is a debt. Loose is a gift. His shoulders stopped holding the invisible backpack of lesson plans and parent conferences and faculty meetings. His hands, which had spent three decades gripping dry-erase markers and pointing at maps and raising themselves for quiet in a room of thirteen-year-olds, opened on the armrests of the Adirondack like two animals who'd been released into a field and weren't sure yet that the fence was really gone.

He looked at the hillside. The hawk was still circling. The oaks were still green. The grass was still gold. Nothing required his interpretation or annotation. Nothing needed a lesson plan. The landscape existed the way landscapes exist when they're not being turned into curriculum — fully, quietly, without a thesis statement.

The black pepper hummed in his chest. The sage grounded him to the porch. The lemon kept his mind bright enough to notice the beauty without trying to teach it to anyone. And the lemongrass and geranium, the softer voices in the blend, did their work at the margins — easing, sweetening, making the whole experience feel less like a moment and more like a climate.

Ellis sat there. The Bonobo track ended and another one began, something with a piano that sounded like rain falling on a surface that was happy to receive it. The September sun moved a degree to the west. The shadow of the porch railing shifted across his bare feet like a slow clock that had nowhere to be.

He thought about Natty Bumppo — the character, not the strain. A man who lived in the forest and needed nothing from civilization except the occasional conversation and a good rifle. A man who was happiest when he was between places, when the trail was under his feet and the sky was overhead and the next clearing was just a possibility, not an obligation.

Ellis was not in a forest. He was on a porch in Redwood Valley with dirty hands and tomato plants and a neighbor who grew biodynamic cannabis and fed snails to chickens. But the feeling was the same. The untethered feeling. The feeling of being loose in your own life, present without performing, happy without needing to name why.

He closed his eyes. Opened them. The hawk had landed on a fencepost across the drainage. It was looking at something in the grass with the calm focus of a creature that had never once worried about retirement.

Ellis smiled.

Not at anything. Not for anyone.

Just because his face, like the rest of him, was free to do what it wanted, and what it wanted, on this particular Saturday, was to simply agree with the afternoon.

## Prose Poem: "Terpene Frontier"

There is a porch in Redwood Valley where the sage on the hillside and the sage in the diffuser are having the same conversation in different dialects, and if you sit long enough with black pepper warmth in your chest and lemon clarity in the air and the geranium floating its strange plum-sweet question above it all, you will remember something you forgot the day you got your first job: that your body, before it was an employee, was an animal, and animals don't need a reason to sit in the sun. Moon Gazer Farms is a half-mile down the road — Josh and Sandra built the soil themselves, hauled dead trees from the forest, layered them with native dirt and chicken-composted calcium in a closed loop that turns snail shells into medicine. Every seed is different. Every plant is a little bit its own thing. That's the philosophy: don't clone, don't control, let each one be what it becomes. Natty Bumppo became this — fourteen drops of black pepper like a handshake from a warmth that means it, ten drops of lemon like a sentence that says exactly one thing and says it clearly, six drops of sage like the hill you can see from where you're sitting, six drops of lemongrass like the part of relaxation that doesn't need to announce itself, and four drops of geranium like the sour plum tartness that makes happiness interesting instead of just sweet. The frontiersman moved through the forest without needing to own it. The retiree sits on the porch without needing to schedule it. The kerosene in the aroma is the earth giving up something ancient. The musk is the body admitting it's warm. The sour plum is joy with a tart edge, the kind that doesn't cloy because it never pretended to be simple. You don't untether yourself. You just stop holding the rope, and discover your hands were the only thing keeping you from the Saturday you were always owed.

## Haiku Triptych: "Three Freedoms of the Frontiersman"

**Inhale**
Black pepper and sage —
the hillside sends its regards
through the diffuser.

**Hold**
Sour plum, kerosene.
Lemon keeps the mind just bright
enough to notice.

**Exhale**
Loose. Not lazy. Just
a body that remembers
it was free all along.

## Sonnet

The sage oil smells like every hill I know —
the dry September slopes behind my home.
The black pepper is warmth without a show,
a steady fire that doesn't need to roam.

The lemon cuts the afternoon to size,
the geranium floats a plum-tart sweetness here.
The lemongrass is soft below the skies
and asks for nothing, which is why I hear.

I taught for thirty years the frontier's song —
how Bumppo moved through woods without a deed.
Today I learn what he knew all along:
that freedom isn't found, it's just agreed.

The hawk lands. The garden's done. The sun is slow.
I'm loose inside my life, with nowhere else to go.

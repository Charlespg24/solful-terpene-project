## Short Story: "The River After"

Maren hadn't been warm in four months. Not really warm — not the kind that goes all the way to the center. She'd been functional-warm, layered-warm, the kind of warm that comes from Gore-Tex and SmartWool and the constant low-grade friction of moving through cold water all day. But bone-warm, core-warm, the kind where your marrow relaxes? Not since May.

She was a river guide on the Eel. Six seasons now. April through September she ran the upper fork out of a base near Dos Rios — class III rapids, some IV when the water was up, school groups and bachelor parties and the occasional quiet couple in their sixties who actually listened when she explained how to read the water. It was the best job she'd ever had and also the most physical, and by October her body carried the season the way a riverbank carries the high-water mark: you could see exactly where the effort had been.

Now it was the first Saturday of October and the last trip had run three days ago and she was in the claw-foot tub in her cabin above the river, in water so hot it had turned her skin the pink of cooked shrimp, and she was not moving.

The cabin was small — one room plus a sleeping loft, a woodstove she'd lit an hour ago, a kitchen that was really just a counter and a two-burner. She'd found it through a friend of a friend five years ago, a rental on a piece of land where the Eel bends south through the canyon, and the sound of the river was so constant that she only noticed it when she left. Tonight it was the background of everything — a low, continuous rush, like the world breathing through a slightly open window.

The diffuser sat on the edge of the tub, balanced on the wide lip of the porcelain. She'd loaded it heavily — this was not a night for subtlety. Lemongrass, sixteen drops, more than she'd ever used, and the smell filled the small bathroom like a green tide coming in. It was thick and sweet and slow, the way lemongrass always was — the myrcene expressing itself as a kind of olfactory gravity, pulling everything downward, making the air itself feel heavier and more willing to hold you. Pine, eight drops, and this was the note that surprised her every time — sharp and bright and briefly clarifying, like a window opened in a warm room, a flash of alpine air that cut through the lemongrass sweetness and said: you're not asleep yet, notice this. Black pepper, six drops, warm and steady, settling beneath the other scents like coals at the bottom of a fire. Basil, six drops, herbal and green and slightly strange, an ocimene note she couldn't quite name but that made the blend feel more complex, more alive, like a forest floor with something growing underneath the obvious. And lemon, four drops, sharp enough to echo the lime she'd smell later, a citrus edge that kept the heaviness from becoming total.

The lemongrass reached her first. It always did when you used that much — it was insistent the way sleep is insistent, the way gravity is insistent, not aggressive but undeniable. It settled into her muscles the way warm water was already settling into her muscles, and the two together — the heat and the scent — began the process that she'd been waiting four months for: the systematic unhurrying of a body that had been required to react quickly every day since April.

Then the pine, cutting through. A brief flash of sharpness, a memory of the forest above the river where the Douglas firs grew so tall they made their own weather. Then the black pepper, warm and grounding, and the basil, green and slightly musky, and the lemon, tart and clean. The blend didn't harmonize so much as layer — each oil occupying its own altitude in the warm, wet air, the lemongrass at the bottom, the pine at the top, everything else finding its place between.

She'd made the tea before getting in the tub. A recipe she'd copied from a card at Solful, the dispensary in Sebastopol she stopped at every time she drove the 101 south. Dried rhubarb, dried lime peel, rosemary, basil, bay leaf, a pinch of cinnamon, agave to smooth the tartness. It sat on the stool beside the tub in a ceramic mug the color of river stone, still steaming. The rhubarb gave it a pink tint and a sour-sweet depth that matched the strain's name in a way that felt less like coincidence and more like conversation.

She reached for the mug. Sipped. The rhubarb tartness hit first — sharp and alive, almost challenging — and then the rosemary and basil softened it, and the lime peel brightened it, and the cinnamon warmed it from underneath, and the bay leaf did something she couldn't name but that made the whole thing taste like it had been steeped not in water but in time.

The jar was on the shelf above the sink, next to her toothbrush and a bottle of arnica. She'd bought it last weekend at Solful. The guy at the counter — different guy from last time, older, glasses, the kind of unhurried speech that made you feel like whatever you were buying was important — had held the jar up to the light and said, "Black Lime Chem. Moon Gazer Farms, out in Mendocino. Josh and Sandra Khan. You know their farm?"

"I know the area. I guide on the Eel."

"Biodynamic," he'd said. "They build their own soil — fallen trees from the forest, native dirt, chicken manure, the whole cycle. Every seed is different. They don't clone." He'd paused. "This one's got the highest myrcene in the collection. Three-point-oh-eight percent total terpenes. It's the heaviest thing we carry." He'd looked at her, assessing. "End of season?"

"Three days ago."

He'd nodded like this explained something. "Then this is yours. This is the one that lets your body stop being a tool and start being a body again."

Now she opened the jar — awkwardly, wet hands, the lid slippery — and the smell rose into the steam. Sharp lime, unmistakable, cutting through the lemongrass haze like a green blade. Rhubarb, tart and earthy, the same note the tea was already singing. And underneath, the glue — dense, adhesive, the myrcene making itself known not as a scent so much as a texture, the olfactory equivalent of something that sticks, that holds, that pulls you gently toward a surface and keeps you there. It smelled like what it did: like being held down by something that meant well.

She packed the small pipe — a glass piece, simple, the color of river jade — and took one draw without leaving the tub. Held it. The smoke tasted like the jar smelled: lime and rhubarb and that dense, glue-sweet bottom note. She exhaled and the smoke joined the steam and the diffuser vapor in the small bathroom, three kinds of warm air becoming one warm air, and the room disappeared into its own atmosphere.

The myrcene hit like a tide.

Not a wave — a tide. Slower than a wave, deeper than a wave, more inevitable. It started in her legs, which had spent six months bracing against river current and slippery rock, and it moved upward, a warmth that was heavier than the black pepper warmth, heavier than the bathwater warmth, a warmth that had weight. Her thighs went first. Then her hips, those joints that had absorbed every portage and every scramble over the boulders at Hamburger Falls. Then her lower back — the place where every paddle stroke had been filed away, a season's worth of effort stored in the muscles along her spine like data in a hard drive that was finally being allowed to shut down.

The pine flashed through — one bright moment of clarity, a window of alertness where she noticed the quality of the light in the bathroom, the amber of the candle she'd lit on the sink, the way the steam made halos around everything, and she thought: this is beautiful, this exact moment, and then the moment passed and the lemongrass closed over it like water over a stone, gentle, total, warm.

Her arms went heavy. Her hands, which had gripped paddles and ropes and the gunnels of overturned rafts, sank into the water and rested on her thighs. Her jaw did the thing jaws do when the myrcene is heavy enough — it didn't unclench so much as it forgot the concept of clenching, as if the muscles on either side of her face had been told they could go home for the winter.

The basil and the lemon continued their quiet work at the edges of the blend — the basil adding a green herbal complexity that kept the heaviness from becoming monotone, the lemon maintaining the faintest razor-edge of sharpness, a reminder that she was still here, still conscious, still in a body that was choosing to be still rather than being forced.

The tea cooled beside her. The river ran below. The woodstove in the next room ticked and sighed as the fire found its rhythm.

She thought about the Eel. Six months of reading water — learning to see the V-shape of the current over a submerged rock, the slick green tongue of a chute, the foam line that told you where the eddy was. All of it required a body in the ready position: leaned forward, core engaged, eyes scanning. All of it required the opposite of what she was doing now.

What she was doing now was the opposite of scanning. The opposite of leaning forward. She was leaning back into water that held her weight without opinion. She was breathing in lemongrass and pine and basil and black pepper and lemon, and each breath was slower than the last, not because she was trying to breathe slowly but because her lungs had recalibrated, had found the rhythm of a body that was no longer on the river but was, at last, being carried by one.

The candle flickered. The steam thinned as the bathwater found its equilibrium with the room. The lemongrass continued its slow green gravity. The pine was gone now — absorbed, metabolized, its clarity donated to the moment and then spent. What remained was the bottom of the blend: the pepper's steady warmth, the basil's herbal whisper, the lemongrass pulling everything gently, inexorably, toward rest.

Maren closed her eyes.

Not to sleep. Not yet. But to feel the specific weight of this moment — the water against her skin, the mug going cool on the stool, the sound of the Eel below the cabin doing what rivers do when no one is watching them, which is the same thing they do when everyone is: moving forward, carrying whatever falls in, turning everything eventually to smooth.

Her body was no longer a tool.

It was a riverbed. And everything that had been rushing through it all season — the cold, the effort, the vigilance, the joy — was slowing now, settling, becoming sediment, becoming the soft, weighted floor that the next season's current would run over without disturbing.

She breathed in lemongrass. She breathed out October.

And the bliss — because that's what it was, not happiness, not relief, but bliss, the specific bliss of a body that has been used well and is now being told it can stop — the bliss settled over her like a second water, heavier than the bath, warmer than the bath, and she let it hold her because that was its only request, and she had spent six months holding everything else.

## Prose Poem: "Terpene Sediment"

There is a cabin on the Eel River where the water runs all night like a lullaby that doesn't know when to stop, and if you fill the tub and load the diffuser with sixteen drops of lemongrass — enough to make the air itself feel heavy, the myrcene pulling everything toward the floor the way gravity pulls a river toward the sea — and then add eight drops of pine for the one flash of clarity you'll need to notice how beautiful the steam looks in the candlelight before the lemongrass closes over that clarity like water over a stone, and six drops of black pepper for the warmth that sits at the bottom of every good rest like coals in a woodstove, and six drops of basil for the green herbal strangeness that keeps the heaviness from becoming monotone, and four drops of lemon for the sharp lime edge that says you are still here, still conscious, still choosing this — if you do all of this, and then open the jar from Moon Gazer Farms where Josh and Sandra build their soil from fallen trees and chicken-composted calcium and native Mendocino dirt and every seed is different because they believe in what's wild, you will smell sharp lime and rhubarb and something dense and adhesive that the word glue doesn't quite capture but your body understands immediately as an invitation to stop — not to stop living but to stop holding, stop gripping, stop bracing against the current — and the myrcene will come for you the way a tide comes for a shore, not aggressive but total, and your arms will go heavy and your jaw will forget it ever clenched and your back will release a season's worth of effort into the water and the water will accept it without opinion, and you will understand, finally, in the only language the body trusts, that bliss is not a feeling you arrive at but a weight you allow, and rest is not the absence of the river but the moment you stop swimming and let it carry you.

## Haiku Triptych: "Three Descents into Black Lime"

**Inhale**
Lemongrass and pine —
one pulls me down, one flashes
clear. Then the green wins.

**Hold**
Sharp lime. Rhubarb. Glue.
The body stops being a tool
and starts being held.

**Exhale**
Heavy. Not broken.
Just the slow tide of a season
finally arriving home.

## Sonnet

The lemongrass is heavy — sixteen drops
of green that pulls like gravity, like tide.
The pine flares once, a clarity that stops
the drift just long enough to look inside.

The black pepper is coals beneath the rest,
the basil adds its strange green herbal hum.
The lemon keeps a sharpness in the chest —
a reminder I'm still here, not fully numb.

The river runs below. The woodstove ticks.
The steam and smoke and vapor merge to one.
My body, done with rapids and with tricks,
becomes the bed the current settles on.

The myrcene pulls. The bliss has weight, not wings.
I rest the way a riverbed just — rests. And sings.

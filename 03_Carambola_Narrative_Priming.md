## Short Story: "Starfruit Morning"

The sunrise came in orange, and Davi said, "See? Even the sky is on theme."

They were parked at the campsite above Albion, the one you have to know someone to know about — no sign, no reservation system, just a clearing in the redwoods where the coast appears through a gap in the trees like a secret being told mid-sentence. Yuki's van, a 1987 Westfalia the color of a sage leaf, was nosed toward the view. The sliding door was open. Two camping chairs sat in the dirt like thrones for people with no ambitions beyond coffee and the next four hours.

It was 7:15 on a Sunday in May, and the air had that Mendocino morning quality — cool enough to justify a sweatshirt, bright enough to make the sweatshirt feel like an event with a time limit. The fog was offshore, a white wall sitting at the horizon like it was waiting for an invitation. It wouldn't get one. Not today. Today was all citrus and clarity.

"Hand me the oranges," Yuki said. She was standing at the fold-out table they'd set up last night, the one with the wobbly leg that Davi had stabilized with a copy of *Braiding Sweetgrass* he swore he'd read eventually. In front of her: a cutting board, a knife, four starfruit she'd found at the Mendocino co-op yesterday, and a small hand-press juicer that she carried everywhere like other people carry phone chargers.

Davi tossed her an orange from the cooler. It caught the early light and for a half second looked like a small sun being passed between friends, which is essentially what it was.

The diffuser was running on the van's fold-down counter — a portable one, USB-powered, the size of a coffee mug. Yuki had loaded it that morning while Davi was still asleep, which meant he'd woken to the smell of sweet orange and rosemary drifting through the van's open windows and mixing with the redwood air. The sweet orange was the lead note — bright and immediate and absurdly cheerful, like a friend who arrives at your door already laughing about something that happened on the way. The rosemary sat underneath, warm and herbal and specific, a steadying presence that kept the orange from becoming giddy. The lavender was barely there — a softness at the edges, like the morning light itself, not asking for attention but making everything it touched slightly kinder. And the German chamomile — Yuki had added it last, six drops that smelled like a garden that forgave you for not visiting — rounded the whole thing into something you could breathe without thinking and think without trying.

The black pepper was the quietest voice, four drops at the bottom of the blend, but Davi noticed it. He always noticed the warmth. It was the part that made the brightness feel lived-in rather than performed.

"Okay," Yuki said, slicing a starfruit crosswise. The slices fell onto the cutting board in perfect five-pointed stars, and she held one up to the light. "Tell me this fruit wasn't designed by someone who was extremely high and also a geometry teacher."

"Carambola," Davi said, because he'd looked it up last night. "That's the real name. Star fruit is just the marketing."

"Carambola," Yuki repeated, letting the word roll around in her mouth. "That's a better name for everything. Not just the fruit. The morning. This whole morning is a carambola."

She pressed the starfruit through the juicer — a pale, slightly tart liquid that smelled like citrus and something stranger, something that didn't have an English word but might have been the flavor equivalent of the color chartreuse. She added fresh orange juice, a splash of cardamom water she'd steeped at the campfire last night, and coconut water from a carton. Poured it over crushed ice into two mason jars.

Davi took his and sipped. It was cold and bright and a little bit weird in a way that made him want to sip again immediately, the way a good joke makes you want to hear it again even though you already know the punchline. The starfruit was tart-sweet-alien. The orange was its friendlier translator. The cardamom added a warmth that sat on the back of the tongue like a small hello from a spice that understood timing.

From the Bluetooth speaker wedged in the van's cup holder, FKJ was doing that thing where a single person somehow sounds like an entire band having the best morning of their lives. A Rhodes piano line that sparkled. A bass that bounced rather than thudded. Something with a flute that had no business being this perfect at 7 AM but here it was, being perfect, not apologizing for it.

"So," Yuki said, settling into her camping chair and pulling her knees up. "The jar."

The jar was sitting on the fold-out table between the cutting board and a half-eaten bag of dried mango. Small glass, white label, the kind of simple packaging that says we spent the money on what's inside. Davi had bought it Friday at Solful, the dispensary in Sebastopol they always stopped at on the way up the coast. The woman at the counter — mid-forties, turquoise rings on three fingers, the energy of someone who genuinely liked Monday mornings — had pulled it from behind the display and said, "Carambola. Higher Heights farm, up here in Mendocino. It's limonene-forward. You know what that means?"

"Citrus?" Davi had guessed.

"Citrus is what it smells like. What it means is your brain gets the memo before your body does. It's a head start on feeling good." She'd smiled. "Think of it as optimism in a jar. The kind that doesn't require evidence."

He'd laughed at that. Bought it immediately.

Now he opened the jar and the smell joined the morning — orange peel and something faintly diesel, like a beautiful machine, and underneath that a whisper of incense, the kind you smell in shops that sell both crystals and very good tea. It met the sweet orange from the diffuser and the fresh-pressed starfruit in the air and suddenly the campsite smelled like a place where every good idea you'd ever had was planning a reunion.

Yuki packed the small glass pipe — a one-hitter she'd bought in Arcata, shaped like a starfish, because of course it was. One draw each. That was the agreement on mornings like this: enough to open the door, not enough to forget where the door was.

Davi inhaled. The smoke tasted like orange zest and motor oil and temple — those three aromas, woven together, the diesel somehow making the orange more orange and the incense making both of them feel ancient and new at the same time. He held it. Let it go. The smoke climbed into the redwood canopy and disappeared the way good thoughts disappear — not lost, just absorbed into something larger.

It arrived fast. Limonene doesn't wait in line. One moment the morning was beautiful in the way mornings are beautiful when you're camping with your best friend and the coffee is working and the sky is clear — the ordinary, adequate beauty of a life going okay. And then, between one breath and the next, the beauty sharpened. Not intensified — sharpened. Like someone had adjusted the contrast on reality and now the bark of the redwoods was more itself, and the ocean in the gap between the trees was a color that blue had been trying to be all along, and Yuki's laugh — she was laughing at something on her phone, or at nothing, or at the starfruit juice, which had left a faint yellow mustache on her upper lip — Yuki's laugh was the best sound any human had ever made, and the fact that this was probably not objectively true made it feel even truer.

"Why is everything—" Davi started.

"I know," Yuki said.

"I didn't even—"

"I *know*."

They sat there grinning at each other like two people who'd just discovered that the morning had a secret level and they'd found the entrance by accident. The sweet orange from the diffuser kept doing its steady, jubilant work. The rosemary anchored the brightness to something real — the smell of the herb garden Davi's grandmother kept in Bakersfield, the warmth of a kitchen where someone was always making something. The chamomile gentled the whole experience, smoothing any edge that might have felt too electric, too much, into something that was exactly enough.

And the lavender — barely there, a suggestion rather than a statement — was the part that made the joy feel safe. Not cautious. Not careful. Just held. The way a good friend holds your laugh: not stopping it, not shaping it, just making sure it has somewhere warm to land.

Yuki picked up her sketchbook. She was drawing the starfruit slices — quick, loose lines, nothing precious, the pen moving like it was having its own good morning. Davi watched the ocean. The fog wall hadn't moved. The FKJ track had shifted into something with a saxophone that sounded like it was smiling.

He sipped the juice. Starfruit and orange and cardamom. Cold and bright and a little bit strange. Like the morning. Like the strain. Like the word *carambola* itself — five syllables that sounded like a celebration in a language you were just beginning to learn, where everything was both unfamiliar and immediately understood.

"What do you want to do today?" Yuki asked, not looking up from her sketch.

Davi considered this. The coast was right there. The redwoods were right there. They had food, they had the van, they had a full tank and no obligations until Tuesday. The question contained every possible answer.

"Exactly this," he said. "But in different locations."

Yuki looked up. Grinned. "Carambola," she said, like it was a toast.

"Carambola," he agreed.

The morning kept going. It didn't need their permission. It just kept offering — light, and warmth, and the particular effervescence of a day that hasn't been planned, in a place that smells like oranges and rosemary and the soft chamomile tenderness of a world that, at least right now, in this clearing, in this light, with this person, required absolutely no improvement.

## Prose Poem: "Terpene Effervescence"

There is a campsite in Mendocino where the redwoods open like a curtain and the Pacific appears like a punchline you weren't expecting but that makes everything before it funnier, and if you arrive on a Sunday morning with starfruit and a friend and a jar from Higher Heights farm, the sweet orange will reach you first — sixteen drops of sunrise in a bottle, the limonene arriving in your prefrontal cortex before your body has finished yawning, telling every synapse that today has no ceiling. The rosemary is next, eight drops of the warmth that lives underneath playfulness, the caryophyllene saying you can be bright without being brittle, the way a campfire is both light and heat. The lavender — six drops of a softness so quiet you might mistake it for the morning air itself — is the part that lets you laugh without flinching, the linalool padding the edges of every sensation so the joy has somewhere soft to land. And the German chamomile, six tender drops of a garden that grows only forgiveness and wildflowers, the bisabolol holding the whole effervescent mess together with a gentleness that says: feel everything, I'll keep watch. Four drops of black pepper at the bottom of the blend, a warmth so subtle you only notice it when it stops, and then you realize it was the floor all along. This is what a carambola morning tastes like — starfruit and orange juice over crushed ice, FKJ on a speaker the size of your fist, your best friend drawing five-pointed stars in a sketchbook while the fog offshore decides not to come in, and every molecule in the air conspiring to make right now the most interesting thing that has ever happened, not because it is, but because for once you are paying attention with your whole body, and your whole body is saying yes.

## Haiku Triptych: "Three Stars of Carambola"

**Inhale**
Sweet orange and dawn —
the redwoods hold still while we
remember to play.

**Hold**
Starfruit geometry.
Rosemary warmth beneath the fizz.
The morning sharpens.

**Exhale**
Bright. Not brittle. Just
the soft hum of two friends who
found the day's frequency.

## Sonnet

The starfruit falls in perfect fives of light,
and sweet orange fills the campsite clearing's air.
The rosemary says warmth beneath the bright,
the chamomile says tenderness is there.

The fog sits on the ocean line, polite,
and knows today it won't be asked ashore.
The FKJ floats upward through the light
like joy that's learned it doesn't need to roar.

I sipped the juice — tart, strange, a little gold —
and felt the morning tighten into focus.
Not sharper. Cleaner. Everything I hold
is realer than the name I give to know it.

The lavender is barely there, but stays —
the softness underneath effervescent days.
